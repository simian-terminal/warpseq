Transforms

Transforms are like meta-patterns - they are specialized patterns that are computed on top of and modify the result of patterns before those patterns are turned into MIDI events. Tranforms take notes listed in patterns inside a Clip and then do "things" to them - they don't just have to change the notes - they can also alter velocity and MIDI CC data. Transforms are optional features to Warp, but very fun, and can add lots of sonic variety.

  • Classically, arpeggiators are used in computer synthesizers to turn chord patterns into individual notes, played in rapid sequence
  • In Warp, transforms CAN be used to create arpeggiators, but that is only the most basic usage of them.
  • Transforms can also run against individual notes (not just chords), and even turn those notes *back* into chords.
    • .
  • Transforms achieve this power through Mod Expressions.
  • Transforms may be thought of as a special kind of Pattern that runs against Patterns.
  • A transform with a divide time of 1 applies each slot of the transform to one slot of the pattern, and only modifies the notes, rather than creating more.
  • A transform with a divide time of 4 will divide each note into four parts, applying up to 4 different modifications to each fragment
  • A transform can contain more slots than the divide time, allowing for arps that do not line up exactly with the notes they modify
  • Transforms can manipulate notes or octaves, can silence notes, and can also include velocity and MIDI CC information.

    • Basic Examples

    Assume we have an incoming chord sequence: 
    
I
IV
V
    And a transform: 0 With a divide cycle of "3". In this simple example - with just one expression - we have built an arpeggiator, that plays the notes of the triad in quick succession.

    A More Complex Example

    Suppose we have the same chord sequence and a different transform: 
    
O+1
0
0
    This will play the first note of each chord (the root) an octave up. For more examples, see the Expression Section.

    With a divide time of 6, we just built a classic two octave arp. It plays the chord, then plays the same chord input an octave higher.

      Transforms can use ANY mod expression listed in this documentation - they just don't start with notes. In the documentation, you may see many useful expressions demoed with starting notes. When this happens, these examples are showing patterns -- but remember, you can use the same tools with transforms! Just leave out the starting note or chord symbols.

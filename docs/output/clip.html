Clip

Users of popular grid-based DAWS (Digital Audio Workstations) like Ableton or Bitwig may be familiar with the concept of Clips. Clips are, simply put, something playable that contains MIDI notes. A Song is what happens when you arrange clips by Scene and Track and play a bunch of clips in a meaningful order.

Basic Features

  • A clip primarily contains a list of one or more Patterns that can be manually triggered to play in sequence
  • A clip belongs to a Scene (horizontally) and a Track (Vertically).
  • Playing a clip will stop any other clips that are playing in the same Track as the clip.
  • A clip may override the tempo on a Scene or Song.
  • A clip may have one or more Scales. These scales are used for each Pattern, but the list of Scales may be of a different length from the list of Pattern.
  • If a clip does not have a Scale assigned, the Scale can come from the Scene, and if not there, the Song.
  • A clip may override the tempo set on the Scene or Song

Advanced Features

  • A clip may have one or more Transforms, which are expressions that modify the currently playing Pattern. These are used in rotation just as the Scales are.
  • A clip may have one or more octave_shifts and scale degree_shifts that apply to the currrently playing Pattern. These rotate similarly. These are easily implemented with mod_expressions and can be removed.
  • A clip may have a list of tempo_shifts, which are added or subtracted to the tempo of each playing Pattern. These rotate similarly.
  • A clip may be set to repeat indefinitely (it will end if the scene ends), or for a finite number of times.
  • A clip may be programmed to advance the Scene when the clip completes. This is off by default.
  • A clip may trigger another specific clip when it completes. This does not occur if the clip is set to advance a Scene.
  • A clip may have a length. If set, Pattern will stop early at this point, rather than using all of the Pattern symbols.

A Simple Clip

A simple clip may conceptually have the following properties: 

tempo : 140
patterns: verse, chorus, verse2, chorus, bridge, verse
scales: f-pentatonic

In this example, the named patterns will play in order. If there was a tempo or scale set on the Scene or Clip it will be overriden by the selections of 140 bpm and the F-pentatonic scale. Since repeat isn't set, the pattern will repeat infinitely until stopped.

A More Complex Clip

Clips can be simple or they can be more detailed. Here's how a maximally complex (and probably non-musical!) example might look. 

tempo: 140
tempo_shifts: [ 0, 10, 20 ]
scales: f-pentatonic, g-minor
octave_shifts, 0, 0, 0, 0, 1, -1, 2
auto_scene_advance: True
repeat: 10
transforms: [ arpeggiate1, stutter2, chaos1 ]
In this example:
  • The base tempo will override the Scene or Song at 140 bpm.
  • The first pattern will actually play at 140bpm, but every 2nd pattern at 150bpm and every 3rd pattern at 150bpm.
  • Every other pattern will alternate between the F-Pentatonic and G-Minor scales.
  • Every fifth pattern will be played one octave higher, the sixth one lower, and the seventh two higher
  • When the clip is finished, the entire next scene will advance.
  • Alternate clips are arpeggiated, sliced and diced, and munged by different transforms, in rotation. See transforms.
  • The sequence of two the two named patterns will repeat 20 times (10 patterns each)

Because the various modifiers have different lengths, nothing will be exactly the same! With transforms and even mod expressions applied to the patterns themselves, this can be even more complex than it looks! But don't worry if this seems advanced, you can use as little features of Warp or as many as you want. It's ok to start slow and learn to make more animated compositions over time.
